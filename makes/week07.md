# Week 7 –  Build a digital map of AI’s infrastructures

## The Artifact
Platform: Google My Maps
Total Locations: 17
Layers: 3 (Headquarters, Data Centers, Data Labor)

This map visualizes the global infrastructure behind artificial intelligence systems developed by Google and Microsoft. The map contains 17 locations across three layers to reveal how power, infrastructure, and labor are geographically distributed in AI development.

The first layer (blue markers) represents corporate headquarters, including Google in Mountain View and Microsoft in Redmond. These locations highlight where major decisions about AI strategy, investment, and governance are made. Concentrating these headquarters in a few technology hubs demonstrates how decision-making power in AI is geographically centralized.

The second layer (green markers) represents data centers, the physical infrastructure required to power cloud computing and artificial intelligence. These facilities are located in places such as Iowa, Oregon, Finland, and Belgium. These locations are chosen because of cheap land, access to renewable energy, reliable electricity, and government tax incentives. Data centers require large amounts of electricity and cooling resources, revealing the environmental footprint behind digital technologies.

The third layer (red markers) represents data labeling and content moderation labor, including locations in Manila, Nairobi, Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Mexico City. These workers perform essential tasks such as labeling images, reviewing harmful online content, and evaluating AI outputs. Their work is necessary to train machine learning systems and make digital platforms “safe,” yet it is rarely visible to users.

The map uses intentional visual hierarchy to show relationships between these layers:

🔵 Blue: Headquarters (decision-making power)

🟢 Green: Data centers (physical infrastructure and energy use)

🔴 Red: Data labor (human labor behind AI)

By placing these layers on a single global map, the visualization reveals a pattern: power is concentrated in a few wealthy technology hubs, while infrastructure and labor are distributed globally.


## Research Documentation
To research the locations included in this map, I used a combination of academic research, investigative journalism, and corporate transparency reports. One key source was Kate Crawford’s Atlas of AI, which examines the physical and environmental infrastructures that support artificial intelligence. Crawford’s work helped frame AI as a global system involving energy, labor, and natural resources rather than purely digital software.

I also used research from the AI Now Institute, which studies the social and political implications of AI technologies. Their reports provided insight into how AI systems depend on large networks of human labor, particularly data labeling and content moderation. For information about data center locations, I referenced infrastructure documentation from Google and Microsoft, as well as reports from Greenpeace’s Click Clean project, which examines the environmental impact of cloud computing.


One of the most difficult aspects of the research was finding reliable information about data labeling labor locations. While technology companies often publicly promote their headquarters and infrastructure investments, they rarely disclose where data annotation or content moderation work is performed.

Another challenge was understanding the environmental scale of data centers. These facilities consume enormous amounts of electricity and water for cooling. Many companies place them in locations with access to renewable energy or government incentives, but the environmental costs are still substantial.

What surprised me most during this research was how distributed the AI system actually is. While public narratives about AI often focus on Silicon Valley innovation, the infrastructure behind AI spans rural regions, global labor markets, and energy systems. Mapping these locations helped reveal how AI is supported by a complex global network that extends far beyond the technology companies themselves.

## Artist Statement
This map argues that artificial intelligence is not simply a digital or virtual technology but a global system shaped by power, geography, and labor. By mapping headquarters, data centers, and data labeling labor associated with Google and Microsoft, the visualization reveals how the benefits and costs of AI are unevenly distributed across different regions.

A clear pattern emerges when these layers are viewed together. Decision-making power is concentrated in a few technology hubs in the United States, where companies control the development and governance of AI systems. Meanwhile, the infrastructure required to operate AI—such as data centers—is distributed across regions where electricity is cheaper, renewable energy is available, or governments offer incentives to technology companies.

At the same time, the human labor that trains and moderates AI systems is frequently outsourced to workers in countries such as the Philippines, Kenya, and India. These workers label images, review harmful content, and evaluate AI responses to help machine learning systems function correctly. Although their work is essential, it is often invisible in narratives that focus only on technological innovation.

This project connects to ideas from Data Feminism, particularly the importance of making hidden systems of power visible through data visualization. Instead of mapping only innovation or corporate success, this map acts as a form of counter-mapping that highlights the infrastructures and labor that sustain AI technologies.

By visualizing these layers together, the map challenges the idea that AI is purely virtual and instead reveals the global networks of energy, labor, and power that make it possible.

## Reflection
When looking at the map, the first thing that stands out immediately is the geographic concentration of corporate headquarters in the United States. While Google and Microsoft operate globally, the centers of decision-making remain in a few powerful technology hubs. At the same time, data centers appear more geographically dispersed, often located in places with cheaper energy or favorable policies.

What is hardest to see on the map is the full scale of the human labor involved in AI systems. Data labeling and moderation work are often outsourced through contractors, and companies rarely publish exact locations or workforce numbers. This makes it difficult to fully map the global workforce that supports AI technologies.

The map also raises several important questions. Who benefits economically from AI development, and who bears the environmental and labor costs? Why are technology companies transparent about data centers but less transparent about outsourced labor? And how might the geography of AI change if governments begin regulating energy use, labor conditions, or data governance more strictly?

These questions suggest that AI is not only a technical system but also a political and economic one shaped by global inequalities.
