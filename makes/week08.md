## Network Analysis 
The most central node in this network is NVIDIA. It connects to multiple major technology companies, including Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Tesla, and acts as a key supplier of AI chips. This makes NVIDIA a critical intermediary between manufacturing (e.g., TSMC) and the companies that deploy AI systems. Its high number of connections and its position between different parts of the network suggest both strong degree centrality and betweenness, meaning it not only has many links but also bridges otherwise separate groups.

Several clusters emerged clearly. First, there is a resource extraction cluster on the left, including cobalt and lithium mines, which are connected to workers and traders. This flows into a manufacturing cluster (CATL, Panasonic, TSMC, Foxconn), which then connects to a Big Tech cluster (NVIDIA, Apple, Tesla, Google, Microsoft, Amazon). Finally, there is an infrastructure and energy cluster centered on data centers and the electric grid. The structure forms a left-to-right supply chain, showing how raw materials eventually become AI systems.

One surprising insight is how small and peripheral the labor and mining nodes appear, despite being essential to the entire system. In contrast, companies like NVIDIA and large data centers dominate visually and structurally, even though they depend heavily on earlier stages of the supply chain.

What is missing from this network is the environmental impact of extraction and energy use, as well as more detailed representation of global inequalities, such as working conditions and regulatory systems. The network simplifies complex global processes and does not fully capture the social and ecological costs that support AI development.

## Artist Statement
This network reveals that power in AI is highly concentrated in a small number of visible actors, particularly large technology companies and infrastructure providers. Nodes like NVIDIA and data centers are positioned centrally and appear larger, emphasizing their dominance in the system. These actors are often the most recognized in public discussions of AI, which reinforces their visibility and perceived importance.

In contrast, the network shows that many essential contributors are structurally and visually marginalized. Workers in mining regions and the sites of resource extraction are pushed to the edges of the network. Although they are foundational to the entire supply chain, they are less connected and visually smaller, making them easier to overlook. This reflects broader patterns of invisibility in global production systems, where labor and environmental costs are often hidden behind technological innovation.

The structure of the network also shapes how we understand power. By organizing the system as a linear flow from extraction to AI deployment, the visualization emphasizes efficiency and progression, but it may obscure feedback loops, dependencies, and inequalities. A different network structure—for example, one that centers labor or environmental impact—would tell a very different story. It could highlight vulnerability, exploitation, or ecological consequences rather than innovation and corporate power.

Overall, this network demonstrates that visualization is not neutral. The choices of what to include, how to connect nodes, and how to position them all influence what becomes visible and what remains hidden in AI systems.
## Process Notes
I created this network using Kumu to visualize the AI supply chain from raw material extraction to AI deployment. I first built a spreadsheet that listed connections between entities, using columns for source, target, relationship type, and weight. This helped me organize how materials, companies, and infrastructure are connected.

After importing the data into Kumu, I structured the network using a left-to-right layout to show the flow from mining (such as cobalt and lithium) to manufacturing (like TSMC and CATL), then to major technology companies, and finally to data centers and energy systems. I chose to use only a few relationship types (extracts, supplies, manufactures) to keep the visualization clear and readable.

I also categorized nodes into groups such as mining, manufacturing, Big Tech, infrastructure, and labor, and used these categories to organize and interpret clusters. Additionally, I sized nodes by degree centrality to highlight the most connected and influential actors in the network. These design decisions helped make the structure of the AI supply chain more visible while keeping the visualization understandable.

## Reflection
This project helped me realize that network visualizations are not neutral representations of reality, but constructed interpretations. While building the network, I had to decide what to include, how to define relationships, and how to position different nodes. These choices directly influenced what appears important and what becomes less visible.

One key insight from my network is how power is concentrated in a few central actors, especially companies like NVIDIA and large data centers. Because they have many connections, they appear larger and more central, which reinforces their importance. At the same time, labor and resource extraction nodes are placed at the edges and appear smaller, even though they are essential to the entire system. This made me realize how visualization can unintentionally reproduce existing power structures by emphasizing already visible actors.

I also noticed that my network simplifies a much more complex reality. For example, it does not fully capture environmental impacts, political influences, or inequalities in labor conditions. These omissions show how networks can obscure important aspects of a system.

Overall, this process changed how I think about data and visualization. Instead of seeing networks as objective, I now understand them as selective representations that reflect certain perspectives. This project made me more aware of how design choices shape meaning and how visualization can both reveal and hide power.
